Motion Model

Our motion model is relatively simple. We are recording linear acceleration and angular velocity in all three axes while someone walks downstairs with a smartphone in their front pocket. We are measuring the oscillatory motion produced when walking down stairs. The magnitudes of the data is less important to us then the frequency of the changes in motion. For a given set of stairs the primary frequency is going to be half the step frequency; the accelerometer cycles with every other step because the smartphone is only on one leg. The secondary frequency is the one that is often more useful in characterizing the set of stairs. For instance, the external West hall stairwells have a turn every half floor, this large change in the vertical angular velocity vector every four-ish seconds appears as a powerful low frequency in the frequency domain of our sensor data.

We chose to collect data from the leg as it has motion through every axis when walking down stairs, unlike the torso which moves less. We made sure to walk consistently and smoothly so as to create data that was as easy to work with as possible.

Algorithm

Our algorithm is relatively straightforward. It takes in trimmed acceleration and angular velocity data, each in three axes, and first applies a low-pass filter with a cutoff frequency of 2Hz. This filtering removes unwanted noise, and ensure that the peak frequencies in the frequency domain are the frequencies that come from the stepping motion and not vibrations produced by impact. Next each data vector is transformed into the frequency domain using the fft and fftshift functions in matlab. We then identify which frequency domain plots are characteristic of the motion. This step was done by hand but could be automated. Frequency domain plots that contain several peak frequencies at similar power levels allow for too much variation in peak frequency between data collections. However, each stairwell chosen has several frequency domain plots that have a very clear singular peak frequency that is easy to identify, these peak frequencies were chosen to be the characteristic frequencies of the location.

A similar process was used in order to identify the stairwell for a test data. The data was filtered and transformed into the frequency domain and the peak frequency was identified for each data axis. These peak frequencies were then compared to the characteristic frequencies of each stairwell and a vector distance was calculated. The lowest distance told us which stairwell our test data was collected at.
